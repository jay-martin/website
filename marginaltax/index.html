Notes

  • It is assumed that all income is labor income.
  • Personal income tax is calculated using the standard deduction.
  • This chart does not take into consideration asset tests (see page: Asinine Asset Tests), work requirements, and wage reductions that can be attributed to the employer FICA tax. SNAP and SSI both include rather onerous asset tests, though these are waived for people who are both categorically eligible for SNAP and live in states that use broad-based categorical eligibilty. This chart does not show SNAP benefits for the small proportion of recipients who are categorically eligible and have gross incomes exceeding 130% of the federal poverty line.
  • Net income for SNAP benefits is calculated using the SNAP standard deduction and the 20% earnings deduction, and does not include additional deductions that some recipients are eligible for. This calculation results in the benefit for one-member households reaching $0 before the gross income eligibility cap. However, additional deductions that some households are eligible for increase the gross income at which one-member households continue to receive a benefit. Additionally, benefit cliffs increase in size if additional deductions are used. See: Appendix: SNAP.
  • Eligibility for SNAP is based on monthly income (excluding those categorically eligible), whereas eligibility for most other programs considered here are based on annual income. To display these together in a single chart based on annual income, it is assumed that monthly income is uniform over the tax year. See: Appendix: SNAP.
  • This chart shows SNAP benefits for people in the 48 contiguous states. SNAP maximum allotments and standard deductions differ in Alaska, Hawaii, Guam, and the US Virgin Islands. SNAP is not available in Puerto Rico, American Samoa, and the Northern Mariana Islands, each of which operate separate food assistance programs.
  • For the purpose of calculating marginal tax rates and benefit values, Medicaid and Premium Tax Credits are treated as one program. This is necessary for continuity, but obscures substantive differences between Medicaid and subsidized private health insurance. See: Appendix: Medicaid and Premium Tax Credits.
  • This chart shows Medicaid under the expansion instituted by the Affordable Care Act. Medicaid has not been expanded in Alabama, Georgia, Florida, Kansas, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Wisconsin, and Wyoming.

Appendix: SNAP

SNAP includes both a gross income limit of 130% of the federal poverty line and a net income limit of 100% of the poverty line. Net income is calculated by subtracting from gross income a number of deductions. This chart includes the SNAP standard deduction ($193 for households with 1–4 members) and the earned income deduction (20% of earnings). Not included are the child support deduction, excess shelter deduction, the dependent care deduction (which includes only expenses related to work or training) and deduction alterations for households with an elderly or disabled member. Note that SNAP has different standard deductions for Alaska, Hawaii, Guam, (all larger) and the US Virgin Islands (smaller). Additionally, some people are eligible for SNAP through categorical eligibility (e.g. people who receive cash benefits through TANF are automatically eligible for SNAP), which does not include a gross income limit. This chart does not show marginal tax rates for the small proportion of SNAP recipients who are categorically eligible for SNAP and have gross incomes above 130% of the poverty line.

When gross income increases by $1, the 20% earnings deduction rises by $0.20. Net income thus increases by only $0.80. Benefits thus decrease by .3($0.80)=$0.24. So a $1 increase in income results in a 24 cent benfit loss, producing an additional income of 76 cents. This is a 24% marginal tax rate. 2022 SNAP Income Limits

The head of household filing status is a family benefit for single parents who pay for more than half of their household expenses. It provides a larger standard deduction and preferential tax rates.

Yes, poor families are excluded from benefits. The HOH filing status does not provide any benefit to single parents who make less than the single standard deduction ($12,950 in 2022). When considered in conjunction with the child tax credit, HOH provides no benefit to single parents with one child with an income below $18,950, to single parents with two children with an income below $24,662, and to single parents with three children with an income below $29,662 (see "How do tax credits affect HOH?"). Furthermore, benefits for working class people are substantially smaller than those for parents with higher incomes (see "Is HOH regressive?").
