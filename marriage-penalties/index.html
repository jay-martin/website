Head of Household

Program Page: Head of Household

The head of household status creates some pretty sizeable marriage penalties. A family of four in which both parents make $30,000, for instance, could save between $798 and $1,596 by getting a divorce, depending on how they claim their dependents. They can reach pretty ridiculous levels at higher incomes (though at higher incomes they probably have less effect on behavior): two heads of households making $80,000 each would incur a penalty of over $6,000 if they married.

It's important to note that it doesn't require careful cherry picking to find these penalties. About x% of marriages are between a childless person and a person with at least one child. If the childless person makes at least $14,000 and the head of household at least $20,000, then they are guaranteed a marriage penalty of over $600 unless one of them is high earner. About y% of marriages are between two people who each have children of their own. In this case, as long as both of them earn at least $19,000 then they are guaranteed to incur a penalty of at least $1,000, with penalties approaching $2,000 common.

The Solution

The way you eliminate the marriage penalties inherent in the head of household filing status is very simple: you eliminate it entirely and plough the savings into a universal child benefit. This takes advantage of a convenient fact: that the single and married filing statuses are already set up in such a way that they do not create marriage penalties.

The Earned Income Tax Credit

Program Page: Earned Income Tax Credit

Constructing an EITC with Minimal Marriage Penalties

The Basic Idea: A marriage consists of two people, each of whom would receive their own EITC if single. Therefore, to prevent marriage penalties, the married EITC should equal the sum of the two individual EITC's each would receive if single.
Click next to start animation
Basics:
We'll start with the single one-child EITC and think about how we'd construct the married
EITC so that marriage penalties are minimized.

Think about two people who are each eligible for the EITC. That's two EITC's.

But if they get married, they'll file their taxes jointly and receive only one EITC.

So if their one married EITC is worth less than the sum of their two individual EITC's,

then they'd lose money as a result of getting married.

Basics:
So the basic idea is this:

When two people get married, their married EITC should be worth the sum of the

two individual EITC's that they would receive if they were single.

Step 1:
First, let's consider how large the maximum married benefit should be.
If two people each receive the maximum individual benefit of $3,000,

then their combined benefit would be $6,000.

Thus, if they get married, they should continue to receive $6,000.

That's double the individual max, so the married EITC max should be twice as large.
Step 2:
Next, consider two people who are each at the very end of the phase-in region.
They each have incomes of $10,000 and EITC's worth $3,000.

So, if they marry, their EITC should equal $6,000 at their combined income of $20,000.
Notice that this simply extends the individual phase-in.

Therefore, to ensure that two people in the phase-in region do not face a marriage penalty,
the married EITC should phase-in at least as fast as the individual EITC up to the new max.
Step 3:
Third, consider two people who each have incomes right at the edge of the plateau.
They each have incomes of $15,000 and receive EITC's worth $3,000.

If they marry, then at their combined income of $30,000 their EITC should be worth $6,000.
$30,000 is twice as large as $15,000, so the married plateau should not end until at least
double the income at which the individual EITC plateau ends.
Step 4:
Finally, consider two people who each have incomes right at the end of the phaseout.
They each have incomes of $30,000, for a combined income of $60,000.

Notice that if their combined income drops below $60,000, then their EITC will be nonzero.

Hence, if they married, they should receive a nonzero EITC of the same value.

Notice that their married EITC should not reach $0 until $60,000 in income.

Thus, the married EITC should not reach $0 until double the income of the individual EITC.

End Result:
A married EITC in which doubling individual income leads to a benefit twice as large. This ensures that any time two people combine their incomes when they marry, their married EITC is at least as large as the sum of their two individual EITC's (at least until one spouse has an income above $30,000).

Reformed EITC

Current EITC

One thing that you'll probably notice is that our reformed married EITC is quite a bit larger than the existing married EITC. The max benefit is worth over $1,000 more and the phaseout is extended substantially. Importantly, this is not because I've tried to slyly fit in a benefit boost for married people—it's simply because this is the bare minimum you need to prevent marriage penalties (except for those intrinsic to phaseouts). Marriage penalties, in other words, save a lot of money. And Congress, it appears, has prioritized saving money over ensuring marriage is not discouraged. Which means that the only way Congress can finally get out of the business of discouraging marriage (without reducing benefits for single people) is to raise benefits for married people.

A problem remains, however. The current EITC is an unwieldy beast, consisting of a total of 8(!) different benefits based on the number of children a filer has. It's an ugly policy that's needlessly convoluted. Just look at it:


This differentiation by number of children makes it really difficult to come up with a coherent benefit schedule that doesn't have marriage penalties. Remember that when we dealt with the one-child EITC, we were really solving for the problem of when a person with one child marries someone else who also has one child, and so they bump up to the married two child EITC. The fact that we have to add together children to determine the married EITC complicates things substantially, because it creates a large number of child combinations. A couple could have three children, for instance, because one partner has three children and the other has none, or because one has two children and the other has one. So our three-child married EITC somehow has to take into account both of these possibilities.

To truly eliminate marriage penalties, the only way to handle this is to deal with the worst case ("worst" in the sense of being the most expensive). So the three child benefit would have to accomodate a two-child parent marrying a one-child parent, because this results in a larger EITC than a three-child parent marrying a childless person. If we didn't do this, and instead created the three-child married EITC around the idea of a three-child parent marrying a childless person, then two child–one child couples could face (pretty substantial) marriage penalties.

At the same time, however, the phase-in for the single three-child EITC is steeper than the phase-in for either the two-child or one-child EITC. Which means that our married three-child EITC is going to need to phase in at least as fast as the single three-child EITC, or else we'd create marriage penalties for three-child single parents who have incomes that put them in the phase-in region.

This necessity to deal with the worst-case itself creates some complications. Notice that if a parent with two children married another parent with two children, then they have four children. The current EITC doesn't have a dedicated married four child EITC, it shoves this married couple into the 3+ children EITC. But if we were to construct the married 3+ children curve around the idea of the couple having three children, then couples with more than three children would face marriage penalties. And as the number of children increases up to six, these marriage penalties grow larger and larger, until they're stupid large. Under the current EITC, for instance, when a parent with three or more children marries another parent with three or more children, they can face marriage penalties of over $10,000!

This means that, if we really want to eliminate marriage penalties, we'll either need to create married EITC's for people with four, five, and six or more children, or we'll have to set the married three+ children EITC at the worst case possibility of 6+ children. The former would cost less but would substantially increase the complexity of the EITC, so I'll consider the latter possibility. Either way, however, the largest married EITC (for 6+ children) is going to have to be much, much larger than the current largest EITC.

All of these considerations together result in the below, ghastly EITC:

The childless EITC is constructed straightforwardly from the process we used above to build a mostly marriage-penalty free EITC (i.e. it's basically "double" the single EITC). The married two-child EITC is exactly the one we constructed in the previous section, only with a steeper phase-in to prevent marriage penalties among two children–zero children couples. The married three or more children EITC shows the worst-case possibility of a parent with three or more children marrying someone else with three or more children. And the married one-child EITC—the EITC for a person with one child who marries a childless person—takes the single one-child EITC and increases the maximum value by $600, the maximum value of the childless EITC.

I hope it is evident from the discussion above that the current structure of the EITC, based around differentiating beneficiaries by the number of children that they have, makes it painfully difficult to build an EITC benefit structure that doesn't have marriage penalties. Frankly, if we want to eliminate marriage penalties, then the current structure is untenable.

Fortunately, there's no law of the universe that says we have to construct it this way. It's just a policy choice, so we can reformulate the EITC along whatever lines we like. And, as I'll explain in the next two sections, it's not only marriage penalties: there are a many reasons to want to restructure the EITC.

Solution 1: Simplifying the EITC

So, you've come to terms with the fact that the EITC as currently structured makes it impossibly difficult to eliminate marriage penalties. You might be feeling sad that such a vaunted policy turns out to be irredeemably flawed, destined to encourage family dysfunction. Now, I will add salt to the wound, and lay out a number of other ways in which the EITC as currently constructed is deeply dissatisfying.

In this section I'll operate within the EITC's basic logic—that it should include a phase-in to "encourage work" and a phase-out to "target" the benefit at low-and-working class people and reduce the sticker price. In the next section, I'll challenge that logic and argue for a more substantial reform.

Along with the infeasibility of eliminating marriage penalties, there are three additional reasons why the current practice of stratiying benificiaires by the number of children that they have is bad:

1. It's needlessly complicated

The tax code is already complicated and confusing.

2. It's incoherent—the EITC is trying to be both a wage subsidy and a family benefit

3. Childless workers receive little—and may even be hurt by the EITC

The Proposal

Proposal 1 is to simplify and rationalize the EITC. We'll collapse it down to just two benefits: a single benefit and a married benefit. We'll construct them as we did in the section above to minimize marriage penalties. This new EITC fixes each of our four problems. It's:

  1. Pro-Marriage: It largely eliminates marriage penalties.
  2. Simple: It makes the program easier to understand, simplifying an already complicated tax code and reducing improper payments
  3. Coherent: It gives the EITC a coherent purpose: it's a wage supplement for working class people, simple as that.
  4. A fair shake: There's no reason to largely exclude low-income childless people from the EITC—they need help too.
  5. Pro-Work: The EITC is designed to encourage work, so it is profoundly strange that we've largely excluded childless people—a large share of prime working age people—from it. The redesign extends the EITC's work encouraging properties to childless people.

Solution 2: Child Benefits & the Standard Credit

There are good reasons to doubt two fundamental elements of the EITC: that it should include a phase-in and a phase-out.

1. The phase-out creates work disincentives

See also: Marginal Tax Rates

The EITC is designed to promote work by topping up the wages people make from work. In the language of economics, the EITC makes use of the substitution effect: by increasing the wage of an additional hour of work, the EITC increases the cost of not working, and thus increases the incentive to work more (to "substitute" additional wages for additional hours of leisure). It must be noted that there is a countervailing income effect—as income rises, people demand a higher quantity of goods, including a higher quantity of leisure–but research suggests that the substitution effect dominates.

In more intutitive language, all this is saying is that if your wage is, say, $15 an hour, then the phase-in region of the one-child EITC increases this to $20.10 an hour. When you're considering whether to work additional hours, that $20.10 an hour makes the additional work more appealing than the $15 an hour wage would.

However, this only holds in the phase-in region. The plateau has no effect on work incentives, and the phase-out reverses the incentives. In the phase out, additional hours of work reduce the amount of the EITC. For example, the one-child EITC phases out at a rate of $210.60 per $1,000 in additional earned income. That means that if you work more and earn an additional $1,000, your EITC decreases by $210.60, and thus your income in fact only rises by $789.40.

This puts the substitition and income effects into reverse. The EITC phase-out reduces additional wages, and thus reduces the cost of not working additional hours, and thus reduces the incentive to work more. Again, the income effect works in the opposite direction, but the substitutiton effect appears to dominate at the incomes at which the EITC phases out.

Another way of saying this is that the EITC phase-out imposes a postive effective marginal tax rate. Other programs, like SNAP and SSI, also impose EMTR's, so they can compuond on top of each other to produce very large effective marginal tax rates: an additional $100 of earned income, for instance, can cause a $21.06 loss in the EITC, and a $24 loss in SNAP benefits, and a $50 loss in SSI benefits. These together create large work disincentives, where additional work nets very little additional money.

The only way to get rid of these disincentives is to reduce or eliminate phase-outs.

2. The only way to truly eliminate marriage penalties is to eliminate the phase-out

3. The phase-out is really just an inferior way to fund the program

4. The phase-in substantially reduces the EITC's poverty alleviation effects

The Standard Credit

The standard credit is not a perfect policy. It has the hallmark problems of a benefit delivered through the tax code rather than through direct spending. You can read about these problems [[[here]]]], and Matt Bruenig has written extensively about them with regards to the child tax credit at the People's Policy Project. In short, tax benefits can end up excluding poor people because people with low incomes are not required to file tax returns, but without a tax return the IRS can't send them a check. This is one reason it's become an increasingly popular proposal to replace the child tax credit, administered by the IRS, with a monthly child benefit administered by the Social Security Administration, which doesn't have this problem.

I'm proposing a standard credit rather than a direct spending program, however, because it strikes me as something that politicians and the general public would be more amenable to. As the political scientists Christopher Ellis and Christopher Faricy demonstrate in their book The Other Side of the Coin: Public Opinion toward Social Tax Expenditures, "public support for a wide variety of social benefits is significantly higher when those benefits are framed as being delivered through tax breaks rather than framed as direct government spending".1

The standard credit fits this mold: it's a "tax cut", not a direct spending program. And as tax cuts go, a standard credit feels in nature a lot like the standard deduction, which is very popular. Additionally, since the standard credit does not necessarily have to replace the EITC—you can easily have both at the same time—a standard credit also offers the alternative pathway of first instituting it as a non-refundable credit, which could make it more amenable to Republicans and the handful of Democratic politicians who are still deeply wed to the idea of phase-ins. Then, with the non-refundable standard credit in place, it becomes a simple and cheap reform to later make it refundable.
