When two people are single, they are each eligible for separate EITC benefits. But when they get married, they are eligible for only one, married joint filer EITC. Because of the structure of the EITC, often times the value of the married EITC is less than the sum of the two individual EITC's. This difference is called a marriage penalty.

Itemized deductions reduce the benefit of the HOH filing status because much of its benefit derives from the larger HOH standard deduction compared to filing as single.

Some very low-income couples incur marriage bonuses. This generally occurs when two people each have children of their own and are in the phase-in region of their respective single EITC's. When they get married, the number of children attributed to their tax unit increases, so they become eligible for a larger EITC curve than either was eligible for before, which can result in a larger benefit than the sum of their individual EITC's. Bonuses can also occur for low-income couples in which one person has a child and the other does not. Bonuses can also occur if one partner has an income close to zero and the other is in the phase-out region of their EITC.

Fixing Head of Household Marriage Penalties

The way you eliminate the marriage penalties inherent in the head of household filing status is very simple: you eliminate it entirely and plough the savings into a universal child benefit. This takes advantage of a convenient fact: that the single and married filing statuses are already set up in such a way that they do not create marriage penalties.

Fixing EITC Marriage Penalties

Eliminating (Most) Marriage Penalties

The Basic Idea: A married couple consists of two people, so the value of the married EITC should equal the sum of two individual EITC's.
Click next to start animation
Step 1:
First, let's consider how large the maximum married benefit should be.
If two people each receive the maximum individual benefit of $3,000,

then their combined benefit would be $6,000.

Thus, if they get married, they should continue to receive $6,000.

That's double the individual max, so the married EITC max should be twice as large.
Step 2:
Next, consider two people who are each at the very end of the phase-in region.
They each have incomes of $10,000 and EITC's worth $3,000.

So, if they marry, their EITC should equal $6,000 at their combined income of $20,000.
Notice that this simply extends the individual phase-in.

Therefore, to ensure that two people in the phase-in region do not face a marriage penalty,
the married EITC should phase-in at least as fast as the individual EITC up to the new max.
Step 3:
Third, consider two people who each have incomes right at the edge of the plateau.
They each have incomes of $15,000 and receive EITC's worth $3,000.

If they marry, then at their combined income of $30,000 their EITC should be worth $6,000.
$30,000 is twice as large as $15,000, so the married plateau should not end until at least
double the income at which the individual EITC plateau ends.
Step 4:
Finally, consider two people who each have incomes right at the end of the phaseout.
They each have incomes of $30,000, for a combined income of $60,000.

Notice that if their combined income drops below $60,000, then their EITC will be nonzero.

Hence, if they married, they should receive a nonzero EITC of the same value.

Notice that their married EITC should not reach $0 until $60,000 in income.

Thus, the married EITC should not reach $0 until double the income of the individual EITC.

End Result:
A married EITC in which doubling individual income leads to a benefit twice as large. This ensures that any time two people combine their incomes when they marry, their married EITC is at least as large as the sum of their two individual EITC's (at least until one spouse has an income above $30,000).

In the module below, you can play around with the EITC outlined above and see that it does, in fact, eliminate all marriage penalties except for those intrinsic to phaseouts. I've also added some ways you could explicitly incorporate marriage bonuses into the EITC, on top of those that already implicitly exist. I personally would not do this—I don't think we should go out of our way to make benefits lower for single people—but perhaps the idea that these "encourage marriage" would make the general reform more appealing to some members of Congress.
