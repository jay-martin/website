Head of Household

The head of household status creates some pretty sizeable marriage penalties. A family of four in which both parents make $30,000 could save between $798 and $1,596 by getting a divorce, depending on how they claim their dependents. While marriage penalties probably have a smaller effect on behavior for higher income couples, they can reach pretty ridiculous levels at these incomes: two heads of households making $80,000 each would incur a penalty of over $6,000 if they married.

It's important to note that it doesn't require careful cherry picking to find these penalties. About x% of marriages are between a childless person and a person with at least one child. If the childless person makes at least $14,000 and the head of household at least $20,000, then they are guaranteed a marriage penalty of over $600 unless one of them is high earner. About y% of marriages are between two people who each have children of their own. In this case, as long as both of them earn at least $19,000 then they are guaranteed to incur a penalty of at least $1,000, with penalties approaching $2,000 common.

The Solution

The way you eliminate the marriage penalties inherent in the head of household filing status is very simple: you eliminate it entirely and plough the savings into a universal child benefit. This takes advantage of a convenient fact: that the single and married filing statuses are already set up in such a way that they do not create marriage penalties.

The Earned Income Tax Credit

Constructing an EITC with Minimal Marriage Penalties

The Basic Idea: A marriage consists of two people, each of whom would receive their own EITC if single. Therefore, to prevent marriage penalties, the married EITC should equal the sum of the two individual EITC's each would receive if single.
Click next to start animation
Basics:
We'll start with the single one-child EITC and think about how we'd construct the married
EITC so that marriage penalties are minimized.

Think about two people who are each eligible for the EITC. That's two EITC's.

But if they get married, they'll file their taxes jointly and receive only one EITC.

So if their one married EITC is worth less than the sum of their two individual EITC's,

then they'd lose money as a result of getting married.

Basics:
So the basic idea is this:

When two people get married, their married EITC should be worth the sum of the

two individual EITC's that they would receive if they were single.

Step 1:
First, let's consider how large the maximum married benefit should be.
If two people each receive the maximum individual benefit of $3,000,

then their combined benefit would be $6,000.

Thus, if they get married, they should continue to receive $6,000.

That's double the individual max, so the married EITC max should be twice as large.
Step 2:
Next, consider two people who are each at the very end of the phase-in region.
They each have incomes of $10,000 and EITC's worth $3,000.

So, if they marry, their EITC should equal $6,000 at their combined income of $20,000.
Notice that this simply extends the individual phase-in.

Therefore, to ensure that two people in the phase-in region do not face a marriage penalty,
the married EITC should phase-in at least as fast as the individual EITC up to the new max.
Step 3:
Third, consider two people who each have incomes right at the edge of the plateau.
They each have incomes of $15,000 and receive EITC's worth $3,000.

If they marry, then at their combined income of $30,000 their EITC should be worth $6,000.
$30,000 is twice as large as $15,000, so the married plateau should not end until at least
double the income at which the individual EITC plateau ends.
Step 4:
Finally, consider two people who each have incomes right at the end of the phaseout.
They each have incomes of $30,000, for a combined income of $60,000.

Notice that if their combined income drops below $60,000, then their EITC will be nonzero.

Hence, if they married, they should receive a nonzero EITC of the same value.

Notice that their married EITC should not reach $0 until $60,000 in income.

Thus, the married EITC should not reach $0 until double the income of the individual EITC.

End Result:
A married EITC in which doubling individual income leads to a benefit twice as large. This ensures that any time two people combine their incomes when they marry, their married EITC is at least as large as the sum of their two individual EITC's (at least until one spouse has an income above $30,000).

Reformed EITC

Current EITC

One thing that you'll probably notice is that our reformed married EITC is quite a bit larger than the existing married EITC. The max benefit is worth over $1,000 more and the phaseout is extended substantially. Importantly, this is not because I've tried to slyly fit in a benefit boost for married people—it's simply because this is the bare minimum you need to prevent marriage penalties (except for those intrinsic to phaseouts). Marriage penalties, in other words, save a lot of money. And Congress, it appears, has prioritized saving money over ensuring marriage is not discouraged. Which means that the only way Congress can finally get out of the business of discouraging marriage (without reducing benefits for single people) is to raise benefits for married people.

A problem remains, however. The current EITC is an unwieldy beast, consisting of a total of 8(!) different benefits based on the number of children a filer has. It's an ugly policy that's needlessly convoluted. Just look at it:


This differentiation by number of children makes it really difficult to come up with a coherent benefit schedule that doesn't have marriage penalties. Remember that when we dealt with the one-child EITC, we were really solving for the problem of when a person with one child marries someone else who also has one child, and so they bump up to the married two child EITC. The fact that we have to add together children to determine the married EITC complicates things substantially, because it creates a large number of child combinations.

For example, if someone with one child marries a childless person, they still have only one child, and so wouldn't bump up to the two-child married EITC that we constructed. Instead, we'd need to construct an additional one-child married EITC. And...it's not immediately clear how we'd do that, because the childless single and one-child single EITC curves are very different from each other. If we're committed to eliminating marriage penalties, the best we can do is create some sizeable marriage bonuses by constructing a confusingly shaped one-child married EITC that takes the one-child single EITC and then increases its maximum benefit by $600 (the maximum benefit of the single childless EITC).

And that's only the beginning of our problems. Above one child, there are multiple ways in which a married couple could end up with more than one child. A married couple with two children could end up with two children because each adult has one child of their own, or because one adult has two children and the other has zero. And a married couple with three children could end up with three children because one parent has three children and the other has zero, or because one parent has two children and the other has one child.

To really eliminate penalties, we have to deal with the worst case ("worst" in the sense of being the most costly, not in the sense that being blessed with new Americans is somehow a bad thing). So the three child benefit would need to accomodate a two-child parent marrying a one-child parent, because this results in a larger EITC than a three-child parent marrying a childless person. If we didn't do this, and instead created the three-child married EITC around the idea of a three-child parent marrying a childless person, then any two child–one child couples would face (pretty substantial) marriage penalties.

But this necessity to deal with the worst-case itself creates problems. Notice that if a three+ child parent marries a three+ child parent, then they have at least six children. Which EITC a person/couple is eligible for is determined by the number of children they have, so does this mean we have to creat a four, five and six+ child EITC? If you actually want to eliminate marriage penalties under the current framework, then basically yes: you either add curves for four, five and six children or you deal only with the worst case and construct a 3+ child married EITC that accomodates families in which each parent has 3 or more children of their own. To keep things somewhat simpler, we'll do the latter.

I hope it is evident from the discussion above that the current structure of the EITC, based around differentiating beneficiaries by the number of children that they have, makes it painfully difficult to build an EITC benefit structure that doesn't have marriage penalties. Frankly, if we want to eliminate marriage penalties, then the current structure is untenable.

Fortunately, there's no law of the universe that says we have to construct it this way. It's just a policy choice, so we can reformulate the EITC along whatever lines we like. And, as I'll explain in the section below, it's not only marriage penalties: there are a great many reasons why we should reformulate the structure of the EITC.
